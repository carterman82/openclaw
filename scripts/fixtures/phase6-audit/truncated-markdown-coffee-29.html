Surface tension is the reason your espresso looks perfect and tastes wrong. That thick, tiger-striped crema you see is mostly air trapped by coffee oils and dissolved solids. It has nothing to do with extraction quality. It is a physical property of the liquid, not a chemical one. A properly extracted shot and a under-extracted shot can both produce identical crema if the pressure and temperature are right. You are judging extraction by a visual cue that measures almost nothing.

This is the single most deceptive signal in home espresso. Baristas have chased crema for decades as a proxy for quality. The specialty-coffee movement taught a generation that thick, persistent crema equals a good shot. It does not. The crema tells you about pressure, grind size, and oil content. It tells you almost nothing about whether the water actually dissolved the right compounds from the coffee.

Consider what crema actually is. It is an emulsion of carbon dioxide, coffee oils, and suspended solids. When hot water forces through a compressed puck at nine bars of pressure, it shears those oils into microscopic droplets. The CO2 released during roasting gets trapped in those droplets, creating the foam. That foam sits on top of your cup and looks like a badge of quality. It is just physics doing what physics does.

The problem is that under-extracted coffee produces crema just as readily as properly extracted coffee. Sour, sharp, thin coffee will give you a thick, tiger-striped crema if your grind is fine enough and your pressure is stable. The oils are still there. The CO2 is still there. The pressure is still nine bars. The crema looks perfect. The cup tastes like fruit vinegar.

That is why your espresso machine produces thick, tiger-striped crema, but the cup tastes sharp and sour.
