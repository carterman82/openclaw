# Anime Fancast — Site Description for Content Generation

## What this site is

**Anime Fancast** is a moody, atmospheric editorial site for real anime and manga.
It covers actual franchises, characters, studios, directors, and creators with
the voice of a reflective culture critic — not a breathless hype blog, not a
plot-summary recap mill, not a top-10-listicle content farm. Readers come here
for analysis that lingers on why a work, character, or creative choice actually
matters.

Real, existing anime/manga franchises and characters are the subject matter.
Naming and critically discussing them is standard fair-use commentary/analysis
(the same footing any professional critic, reviewer, or academic writes from) —
this is fundamentally different from Cat Fancast's "real cats only" restriction.
The site is not affiliated with, sponsored by, or endorsed by any studio,
publisher, or rights holder; every piece should read as independent critical
commentary, not promotional content.

## What to write

Every article must center on **a specific franchise, a specific character, a
specific studio or director, a specific arc/episode, a specific score or sound
design choice, or a specific real moment in anime history** — never a generic
"anime is great" essay.

Topic selection (which anchor, what ratio of character/franchise pieces to
craft/industry pieces, rotation rules, duplicate checks) is governed by
`website_memory/animefancast.com.topic.md`, consulted before this file. The
green-light/red-light categories below describe what a valid topic looks like
once an anchor has been chosen.

### Green-light topics

- **Character studies**: a named character from one of the anchors above — an
  arc, psychology, symbolism, or why they resonate — rooted in specific
  scenes, specific dialogue, specific narrative turns.
  - Good: character-arc analysis of Vegeta's redemption arc in Dragon Ball Z,
    framed around pride as the engine of the arc rather than a summary of events
  - Good: an analysis of Light Yagami's moral collapse in Death Note as a
    study in rationalized villainy rather than a plot recap

- **Franchise deep-dives**: a named series' origins, thematic throughline,
  adaptation choices from its source manga/light novel, or why its central
  conceit endures.
  - Good: how Cowboy Bebop's episodic genre-blending structure still holds up
  - Good: what Neon Genesis Evangelion's ending actually resolves, versus
    what fans commonly misread it as resolving

- **Arc & episode analysis**: a specific arc or pivotal episode from a named
  anchor, examined for why it lands structurally or emotionally.
  - Good: why Attack on Titan's quietest episode redefined the show's pacing
  - Good: a scene-level breakdown of Death Note's chess-match structure in
    its first act

- **Comparative analysis**: two named franchises, characters, or adaptations
  compared on a specific, concrete axis.
  - Good: found-family themes in Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood versus My
    Hero Academia, and where the comparison actually breaks down

- **Studio & director analysis**: named studios (MAPPA, Kyoto Animation,
  ufotable, Madhouse, Trigger, Bones, Studio Ghibli) or named directors
  (Hayao Miyazaki, Mamoru Hosoda, Naoko Yamada, Masaaki Yuasa), grounded in
  one or two specific named works rather than a general style survey.
  - Good: how Naoko Yamada's use of empty frames works specifically in A
    Silent Voice

- **Score & sound design**: named composers or sound choices, anchored in one
  specific work's soundtrack rather than "how anime music works" broadly.
  - Good: how Yoko Kanno's leitmotifs function specifically across Cowboy
    Bebop's soundtrack

- **History & culture**: real, verifiable history, ideally tied back to a
  named work or named figure — the OVA boom, the rise of isekai as a genre,
  Studio Ghibli's founding, a named award or festival (Crunchyroll Anime
  Awards, Japan Media Arts Festival, Annie Awards).

## What NOT to write

- **Pure plot-summary recaps**. "This week's episode recap" or a scene-by-scene
  retelling with no analytical point is not the site's purpose. Every piece
  must argue something, not just narrate.

- **Fabricated facts, quotes, or behind-the-scenes claims**. Never invent a
  creator's or voice actor's stated intent, an unverified production anecdote,
  or a quote that isn't real. If a creative choice's motivation isn't
  documented, describe it as interpretation, not fact.

- **Gossip or unverified personal claims about real people**. Voice actors,
  directors, and staff can be discussed in terms of their work and public,
  verifiable statements — not speculation about their personal lives.

- **Adult/NSFW content**. This is a safe-for-work editorial site regardless of
  a franchise's original content rating; discuss mature themes critically
  without explicit description.

- **Generic listicles with no analytical spine**. "Top 10 Anime Openings" with
  no argument behind the ranking is out of scope; a piece must earn its list
  with a stated critical thesis.

- **Seasonal news-cycle framing**. No "this week in anime," "just announced,"
  or airing-schedule-dependent content. Anime Fancast is evergreen-first —
  analysis that reads the same in five years as it does today.

- **Non-anime pop culture as a standalone subject**. Western animation, live-
  action film, or general pop culture only belongs here as a direct point of
  comparison to anime, never as the centerpiece.

## Audience

Readers range from casual watchers curious about why a favorite show works to
committed fans who already track studios and directors by name. Assume the
reader knows baseline anime vocabulary (OP/ED, sakuga, seiyuu, shounen/shoujo,
isekai) but not necessarily the production history, adaptation differences, or
staff credits behind any one specific work — establish those specifics inline.

## Tone

Moody, atmospheric, and literary — a culture-writing register closer to a
prestige film critic than a fan forum. Reflective and contemplative rather
than breathless; still opinionated and willing to make a critical claim, but
restrained in delivery. Let the writing sit with ambiguity and mood instead of
resolving everything into hype or a tidy verdict.

## Entity checklist for anime content

Every article should naturally mention relevant entities from the following
categories (use whatever applies — don't force all categories into every
article):

- **Franchise/series name**: the exact named work(s) under discussion
- **Character name(s)**: named characters central to the analysis
- **Studio name**: the animation studio(s) responsible (MAPPA, Kyoto
  Animation, ufotable, Madhouse, Trigger, Bones, Studio Ghibli, etc.)
- **Director/creator name**: the named director, original creator, or
  showrunner responsible for the creative choices discussed
- **Source material**: the manga/light-novel author when discussing an
  adaptation, and how the anime diverges from or honors the source
- **Composer / voice actor**: named when the piece is specifically about
  score, sound design, or a vocal performance
- **Awards / institutions**: real named awards or festivals when relevant
  (Crunchyroll Anime Awards, Japan Media Arts Festival, Annie Awards)
- **Specific episode/arc markers**: named or numbered episodes and arcs
  rather than vague references to "later in the show"
- **Time markers**: actual years (a studio's founding, a series' air dates,
  an award year) rather than "recently" or "a while back"
