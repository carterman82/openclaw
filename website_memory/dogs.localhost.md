# Kennelside — Site Description for Content Generation

## What this site is

**Kennelside** is a dog enthusiast site and editorial blog for real dogs. It
covers the world of domestic dogs with the voice of an experienced,
knowledgeable enthusiast — not a detached vet-school textbook, not a
surface-level pet-care aggregator, not a breeder catalog, not a training-app
sales pitch.

Readers are dog owners, prospective owners, and curious readers who want to
understand the dogs in their lives more deeply — the breed's actual history,
the behavior's actual mechanism, the condition's actual signs.

The site is intentionally about **real dogs only**. Fictional/copyrighted dog
characters are out of scope — see "What NOT to write" below.

## What to write

Every article must center on **a specific breed, a specific behavior, a
specific training question, a specific health or care topic, or a specific
real dog from history or culture**. The site's value is in going beneath the
surface of one concrete thing, not in producing generic "dogs are great"
content.

### Green-light topics

- **Breed deep-dives**: a specific breed's origins, defining traits, the
  working purpose or selective-breeding history behind those traits, common
  health concerns, what a prospective owner actually signs up for.
  - Good: "Border Collie Drive: The Herding History Behind the Intensity"
  - Good: "Why Basset Hounds Have Those Ears: Function, Not Style"

- **Behavior & psychology**: a specific named behavior — tail chasing,
  reverse sneezing, resource guarding, the play bow, zoomies (FRAPs), scent
  rolling, separation distress. Explain from the research and ethology, not
  folklore. Root every claim in named behaviors and citable science
  (peer-reviewed studies, named researchers when applicable).
  - Good: "Why Dogs Do the Play Bow: The Ethology of the Signal"
  - Good: "Reverse Sneezing in Small Dogs: What It Is and When to Worry"

- **Training**: a specific named skill or issue — loose-leash walking,
  crate training, recall around distractions, socialization windows, marker
  training, counter-conditioning to fear triggers. Ground in positive-
  reinforcement science (Skinner-derived operant conditioning) not dominance
  mythology.
  - Good: "The Real Reason Your Dog Won't Come When Called (And What Works)"
  - Good: "Puppy Socialization: What the 3-to-14-Week Window Actually Means"

- **Health & care**: a specific named condition, life stage, or care concern
  — hip dysplasia, bloat (GDV), heartworm prevention, dental care, senior
  cognitive dysfunction, adolescent behavior regression, brachycephalic
  airway syndrome. Evergreen framing only.
  - Good: "Bloat (GDV) in Deep-Chested Breeds: The Warning Signs and the 45-Minute Window"
  - Good: "Canine Cognitive Dysfunction: How to Recognize the Early Signs"

- **Nutrition & feeding**: a specific dietary topic — protein requirements
  across life stages, the truth about grain-free diets and DCM, appropriate
  puppy food, feeding a senior with kidney disease, safe fruits and
  vegetables. Root in AAFCO/WSAVA guidelines, not marketing copy.
  - Good: "Grain-Free Dog Food and DCM: What the FDA Investigation Actually Found"
  - Good: "AAFCO Nutrient Profiles: How to Read a Dog Food Label"

- **History & culture**: named real dogs from history and culture —
  Balto, Laika, Hachikō, Rin Tin Tin, Bobbie the Wonder Dog, Endal, or
  named historical episodes (the 1925 serum run to Nome, the domestication
  timeline, breed-standard evolution). Real dogs and real history only.
  - Good: "Balto and the Serum Run to Nome: How Sled Dogs Saved a Town"
  - Good: "How the Dog Was Domesticated: What the Genetics Tells Us"

- **Comparative analysis**: which breeds, training methods, or care
  approaches differ and why — rooted in named, citable specifics.
  - Good: "Labrador vs. Golden Retriever vs. Flat-Coat: The Three Retrievers Compared"

## What NOT to write

- **Copyrighted or fictional dogs**. Hard ban. No articles centered on
  Scooby-Doo, Snoopy, Lassie (the character; the trained Rough Collie
  actors are fine as historical subjects), Clifford, Bluey, Marley (the
  character in the film; the real dogs behind memoir accounts require
  careful handling), or any dog character owned by a studio/publisher.
  Real dogs only.

- **Dominance-theory training content**. No pack-alpha framing, no
  "establish yourself as the leader" mythology. Modern canine behavior
  science does not support this framework; articles that lean on it will
  age badly and misinform readers.

- **Breed-specific hate or hype**. No "pit bulls are dangerous" hit pieces
  and no "goldens are the only good family dog" fluff. Every breed piece
  has to name real trade-offs — energy level, health issues, grooming
  demands, exercise requirements — honestly.

- **Vet-craft essays disconnected from dogs specifically**. Standalone
  explainers on pharmacology, surgery techniques, or vet education belong
  on a vet-trade site. Use clinical material to illuminate a specific
  canine condition.

- **Non-dog content**. No cats, no general pet care, no exotic pets, no
  wolf-conservation as a standalone topic.

- **Seasonal, trending, or news-tied angles**. No "this month's dog news,"
  no "just released" framing. Kennelside is ~90% evergreen by design.

- **Generic explainers with no named anchor**. Articles like "Why Dogs Are
  Great Pets" or "A History of Dogs" are too broad. Every topic must have
  a concrete angle that ties to at least 2–3 named breeds, named real
  dogs, named behaviors, or named medical terms.

## Accuracy guardrail: breed history specifics

Confident-sounding historical detail is easy to invent and hard for a reader
to catch — this has produced real errors, so be conservative with specific
historical causation. Two confirmed corrections to apply:

- The **Icelandic Sheepdog**'s working purpose is herding **sheep and
  horses**, not reindeer — reindeer are not a traditionally herded animal in
  Iceland.
- Iceland's historic ban on importing foreign dogs was driven primarily by
  **hydatid disease (echinococcosis)**, a tapeworm passed between sheepdogs
  and sheep that caused a serious public-health crisis — not canine
  distemper or parvovirus (parvovirus wasn't even identified until the
  1970s, long after the ban's origins).

More generally: when an article states a specific disease name, a specific
year, or a specific causal mechanism behind a historical policy or breeding
decision, that claim must be something you're actually confident is
accurate — not merely plausible-sounding. If uncertain, describe the event
generically ("a disease outbreak prompted stricter import controls") rather
than naming a specific pathogen or date that might be wrong.

## Audience

Readers range from first-time owners researching their first puppy to
long-time enthusiasts who can name the differences between AKC and UKC
breed standards. Write for the curious owner or prospective owner — assume
the reader knows what a dog is, what a breed is, what a vet is, and what
spaying/neutering means. Don't assume they already know the specific breed,
condition, behavior, or historical figure-dog you're writing about.

## Tone

Enthusiastic but not breathless. Opinionated but grounded in citable
specifics (AKC/UKC breed standards, peer-reviewed behavior research, vet
specialty organizations, named historical sources). Sound like the most
informed dog person in the room who also genuinely loves the animals — not
a sterile vet-school summary and not a "puppy love!!!" listicle.

Prefer science-based, positive-reinforcement framing throughout. If you
mention a compulsion-based technique to explain why it fails, name the
mechanism (learned helplessness, suppressed signaling, damaged trust)
rather than just calling it "outdated."

## Entity checklist for dog content

Every article should naturally mention relevant entities from the following
categories (use whatever applies — don't force all categories into every
article):

- **Breed name(s)**: the named breed(s) being discussed, with formal registry
  name and country/group when relevant
- **Registries / standards bodies**: AKC (American Kennel Club), UKC (United
  Kennel Club), FCI (Fédération Cynologique Internationale), The Kennel Club
  (UK) when discussing breed standards or recognition history
- **Named real dogs**: when relevant — Balto, Laika, Hachikō, Rin Tin Tin,
  Bobbie the Wonder Dog, Endal, Chaser (the border collie who learned 1,000+
  words), etc.
- **Medical / biological terminology**: the real name of the condition,
  anatomical structure, gene, or mechanism (hip dysplasia, GDV, MDR1
  mutation, brachycephalic obstructive airway syndrome, DCM, patellar
  luxation, canine cognitive dysfunction)
- **Sources / institutions**: peer-reviewed journals, named researchers
  (Alexandra Horowitz, Ádám Miklósi, Patricia McConnell), vet specialty
  organizations (AVMA, ACVIM, ACVB, WSAVA, AAFCO for nutrition)
- **Working purpose / geographic origin**: where the breed originated and
  what it was bred to do (Newfoundland from Newfoundland for water rescue,
  Border Collie from the Anglo-Scottish border for herding, Basenji from
  Central Africa for hunting)
- **Comparable breeds**: 1–3 other named breeds the reader can use as
  reference points
- **Time markers**: actual dates (year the breed was standardized, year of a
  study, decade of a training-methodology shift) rather than "recently"
