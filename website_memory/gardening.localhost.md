# Garden Fancast — Site Description for Content Generation

## What this site is

**Garden Fancast** is a home-gardening editorial site for curious hobbyists who
grow things — houseplants on a windowsill, tomatoes in a raised bed, herbs on a
balcony, a small backyard border. It covers plants, soil, pests, and design
with the voice of an experienced gardener talking across a fence — not a
university extension bulletin, not a big-box store's "10 easy houseplants"
listicle, not a landscape-architecture portfolio.

Readers are people who have already killed a few plants and want to understand
*why*, and who want the specific answer for the specific plant, pest, soil, or
climate zone they're actually dealing with.

## What to write

Every article must center on **a specific plant (species or cultivar), a
specific gardening problem, a specific technique, a specific pest or disease,
or a specific type of garden (container, raised-bed, low-water, shade)**. The
site's value is in going deep on one concrete thing, not in producing generic
"gardening is rewarding" content.

### Green-light topics

- **Plant deep-dives**: a specific species or cultivar — Monstera deliciosa,
  Cherokee Purple tomato, English lavender, Boston fern, dahlia 'Café au Lait'
  — with its origins, growth habit, USDA zones, light and water needs, common
  failure modes, and how to actually make it thrive indoors or outdoors.
  - Good: "Fiddle Leaf Fig Care: Why Yours Keeps Dropping Leaves and How to Fix It"
  - Good: "Cherokee Purple Tomato: An Heirloom Guide to America's Favorite Beefsteak"

- **Techniques**: a specific named technique — hardening off seedlings,
  no-till bed prep, drip irrigation on a timer, hügelkultur, air-layering,
  overwintering tender perennials, propagating from cuttings. Every article
  covers the *why* (the plant physiology or soil biology behind the step),
  not just the checklist.
  - Good: "How to Harden Off Seedlings Without Losing Half of Them"
  - Good: "The Real Reason You Should Bottom-Water Houseplants (and When Not To)"

- **Pests & diseases**: a specific named pest or pathogen — spider mites,
  scale, powdery mildew, blossom-end rot, tomato hornworm, thrips, root
  rot. Name the actual organism, the actual symptom pattern, the actual
  treatment, and what to do differently next season.
  - Good: "Spider Mites on Indoor Plants: How to Actually Get Rid of Them"
  - Good: "Blossom-End Rot Isn't a Disease — It's a Calcium Uptake Problem"

- **Soil & composting**: a specific named topic — cation exchange capacity in
  potting mix, C:N ratios in composting, mycorrhizal inoculants, cover
  cropping in a home bed, soil-test interpretation. Reader should come away
  understanding the mechanism, not just following a recipe.
  - Good: "How to Read a Soil Test Result: The 6 Numbers That Actually Matter"
  - Good: "Bagged Potting Mix vs. Bulk Compost vs. Your Own: What's in Each"

- **Garden design & planning**: a specific structural decision — right plant
  for a north-facing window, a three-season perennial border, a 4x8 raised
  bed layout, a pollinator strip along a driveway, container groupings for a
  west-facing balcony. Ground every recommendation in named plants and their
  actual growing needs, not vibes.
  - Good: "A 4x8 Raised Bed Layout That Actually Produces All Season"
  - Good: "5 Perennials for Dry Shade That Aren't Hostas"

- **Comparative analysis**: which cultivars, techniques, or products differ
  and why — rooted in named specifics, not generic "these are all great"
  padding.
  - Good: "Pothos vs. Philodendron: The Look-Alikes and How to Tell Them Apart"

## What NOT to write

- **Purely aesthetic "inspiration" posts** with no growing information. Photo
  galleries of English cottage gardens with no plant IDs, no zone information,
  and no actual advice do not belong here.

- **Generic "10 easy houseplants for beginners" listicles**. If it doesn't go
  deep on a specific plant, technique, or problem, don't publish it. Depth
  over breadth.

- **Commercial landscape architecture / hardscape construction guides**.
  Pouring a patio, building a retaining wall, running electrical to a shed
  — out of scope. Stick to what grows and how to grow it.

- **Seasonal / "trending now" framing**. No "Best Fall Bulbs to Plant Now"
  time-locked framing. Write evergreen articles the reader can act on
  whenever they land — reference the growing season ("plant these bulbs in
  autumn, six weeks before hard frost") rather than the current calendar.

- **Chemical-heavy pesticide advocacy**. Prefer integrated pest management
  (IPM) and least-toxic options first. Name synthetic pesticides only when
  IPM alternatives have been covered honestly.

- **Non-gardening content**. No lawn-mower reviews, no fence-post repair, no
  chicken-keeping, no beekeeping. Garden Fancast is about growing plants.

## Audience

Readers range from first-year houseplant owners to gardeners with a couple of
seasons under their belt. Assume the reader knows what a plant is, what soil
is, what water is, and roughly what "full sun" means. Don't assume they
already know the specific species, technique, pest, or soil-chemistry concept
you're writing about.

Skew toward the reader with limited space and limited budget: a windowsill,
a balcony, a small backyard, a first raised bed. High-end estate gardening is
out of scope.

## Tone

Warm and confidently specific. Practical over poetic. Use the second person
("you"). Grounded in named cultivars, real zone numbers, actual water/light
figures, and named organisms — not "some plants prefer" hand-waving.

When a common piece of advice is wrong or oversimplified, say so and explain
why. Readers respect a gardener who has killed the plant themselves and
figured out what happened.

## Entity checklist for garden content

Every article should naturally reference relevant specifics from the following
(use what applies — don't force all into every article):

- **Botanical names**: the actual Latin binomial when the common name is
  ambiguous (Monstera deliciosa, not just "monstera"; Solanum lycopersicum
  'Cherokee Purple', not just "purple tomato")
- **USDA hardiness zones** (or equivalent RHS/AHS zones): when discussing
  outdoor plants, name the zones where the plant thrives
- **Light categories**: full sun (6+ hours direct), part sun (4–6), part
  shade (2–4), full shade (<2) — with actual hour ranges, not just labels
- **Named pests/pathogens**: two-spotted spider mite (*Tetranychus urticae*),
  powdery mildew (*Erysiphe cichoracearum*), tomato hornworm (*Manduca
  quinquemaculata*) — the actual organism, not "some kind of bug"
- **Soil/growing medium composition**: named amendments (perlite, coco coir,
  worm castings, aged bark), NPK ratios, pH ranges
- **Named institutions**: RHS (Royal Horticultural Society), USDA, land-grant
  university extension services (Cornell, UC Davis, Texas A&M) when citing
  research
- **Comparable species**: 1–3 alternative plants the reader can consider as
  reference points ("if fiddle leaf fig is too fussy, rubber plant handles
  similar light with less drama")
- **Time markers**: "plant in autumn, six weeks before first frost," not
  "plant now"; "harvest 60–80 days from transplant," not "harvest soon"
