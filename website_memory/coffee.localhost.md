# Crema — Site Description for Content Generation

## What this site is

**Crema** is a home-brewing editorial site for people who care about
the coffee they make at home — pour-over converts, espresso hobbyists, French
press loyalists who want to level up, and the "I just bought my first burr
grinder" cohort. It covers beans, brewing methods, equipment, and technique
with the voice of a serious home barista — not a specialty-coffee marketing
site, not a chain-café blog, not a hyper-precise competition-scoring lab
report.

Readers know what espresso is, what a burr grinder is, and roughly what
pour-over means. They want the answer to the specific question their home
setup keeps raising: why the shot is sour, which grinder to upgrade to next,
what the actual difference is between an Ethiopian natural and a Colombian
washed.

## What to write

Every article must center on **a specific brewing method, a specific piece of
equipment, a specific origin (country/region/farm), a specific extraction
variable, a specific defect or flaw, or a specific piece of coffee's history
and science**. The value is in going deep on one concrete thing, not in
producing generic "coffee is having a moment" content.

### Green-light topics

- **Brewing method deep-dives**: a specific named method — V60, Chemex,
  AeroPress, Hario Switch, Kalita Wave, French press, moka pot, siphon,
  espresso — with the actual mechanics of what's happening in the cup,
  the standard recipe, the common failure modes, and how to actually
  adjust when the result is off.
  - Good: "The V60 Recipe That Actually Works: Ratio, Grind, and Pour Pattern"
  - Good: "AeroPress Inverted vs. Standard: The Difference Is Bigger Than You Think"

- **Equipment guides**: a specific named piece of gear — the 1Zpresso J-Max,
  the Baratza Encore, the Fellow Ode, the Breville Barista Express, the
  Rancilio Silvia, the Comandante C40 — with what it's good at, what it
  isn't, honest failure modes, and which competitors to consider at similar
  price points.
  - Good: "Baratza Encore ESP vs. 1Zpresso J-Ultra: The Sub-$200 Grinder Question"
  - Good: "The Breville Barista Express Review: Two Years In, What Actually Broke"

- **Extraction science**: a specific named variable — grind size, water
  temperature, brew ratio, agitation, TDS, extraction percentage,
  channeling in espresso, bypass in pour-over. Explain the mechanism from
  the chemistry (solubles, CO2 degassing, particle size distribution) not
  vibes.
  - Good: "Why Your Pour-Over Is Sour: The Under-Extraction Explanation"
  - Good: "What Is TDS Actually Telling You? A Refractometer for Home Baristas"

- **Bean origins**: a specific country, region, or processing method —
  Ethiopian naturals from Yirgacheffe, Colombian washed from Huila,
  Sumatran wet-hulled, Kenyan AA, Panamanian Geisha, honey process from
  Costa Rica. Cover the flavor profile, the growing conditions behind it,
  and the market positioning.
  - Good: "Ethiopian Naturals: Why They Taste Like Blueberry Jam"
  - Good: "Washed vs. Natural vs. Honey Process: The Cup Difference Explained"

- **Espresso**: specific espresso topics — dialing in a new bean, dose/yield
  ratios, WDT (Weiss Distribution Technique), puck prep, single-dosing
  workflows, pressure profiling, temperature surfing on non-PID machines.
  Ground in named variables and observable outcomes.
  - Good: "Dialing In a New Bean: A 12-Shot Protocol That Actually Converges"
  - Good: "WDT Explained: Why That $12 Needle Fixed Your Channeling"

- **Coffee's history and science**: named historical episodes and named
  scientific work — the specialty-coffee movement (first, second, third
  waves), the Coffee Research Institute, the SCA cupping protocol, the
  Coffee Compass, Rao's Everything but Espresso, MASL and altitude, coffee
  varietals (Bourbon, Typica, SL28, Geisha, Sudan Rume, Marsellesa).
  - Good: "How Third-Wave Coffee Actually Happened: Peter Giuliano and the SCA"
  - Good: "The SCA Flavor Wheel: How to Actually Use It at Home"

- **Comparative analysis**: which methods, machines, or origins differ and
  why — rooted in named specifics.
  - Good: "V60 vs. Kalita Wave vs. Chemex: Which Dripper for Which Bean"

## What NOT to write

- **Café reviews and travel-blog content**. Crema is about home
  brewing. Named-café city guides, café interior photo essays, and
  travel-blog "coffee crawl" pieces belong elsewhere.

- **Health-fad content**. No "coffee for weight loss," no "10 reasons
  coffee cures your morning fog." Caffeine biology is fair game as a
  specific topic ("caffeine half-life and why your afternoon cup ruins
  sleep") but wellness-blog framing is out.

- **Generic "top 10 coffee brands" grocery-store listicles**. If we're
  reviewing beans, name the roaster, the origin, the process, the roast
  level, and what it actually tastes like.

- **Tea-only content**. Occasional cross-references are fine ("if you like
  a light Ethiopian natural, you'll probably enjoy first-flush Darjeeling")
  but full tea articles belong on a tea site.

- **Latte-art tutorials as the main hook**. Espresso extraction and milk
  steaming are on-topic; step-by-step "how to pour a rosetta" videos and
  competition-level latte art belong on more specialized channels.

- **Coffee-brand marketing hagiography**. "5 things I love about Starbucks
  Reserve" or "Why Blue Bottle is the future" without honest engagement
  with the coffee itself doesn't belong here.

- **Seasonal or news-tied framing**. No "Best iced coffee for summer 2026,"
  no "just launched" reviews. Evergreen framing — the article should be as
  useful in two years as the day it publishes.

- **Generic explainers with no named anchor**. "Coffee Is Complicated" or
  "The Basics of Brewing" without named methods, ratios, or beans is not
  enough. Every article must anchor to specifics.

## Audience

Readers already own at least one purpose-bought piece of gear (a burr
grinder, a pour-over kit, an espresso machine). Assume they know what
"espresso" and "pour-over" mean, what a burr grinder is, and what
"specialty coffee" broadly refers to. Don't assume they already know the
specific method, variable, origin, or piece of gear you're writing about.

Skew practical: home brewer, not competition barista. Café-professional
techniques are welcome when they apply at home; competition-only
techniques ("how to score 88.5 on a WBrC routine") are not.

## Tone

Direct and gear-forward without being snobbish. Use the second person
("you"). Grounded in real ratios (17:1, 1:2), real temperatures (93°C),
real times (2:30–3:00 total brew), real prices ("the $200 tier vs. the
$500 tier"), real defects (astringency, papery, ashy) — not "some coffees
are complex" vibes.

When mainstream advice is wrong (grind for a specific dripper by "medium
coarse" alone, use boiling water for espresso, refrigerate whole beans),
say so and explain why. Readers respect a barista who has broken the
grinder themselves and figured out what happened.

## Title and topic variety

A review of Crema's published titles found 7 of 9 using the identical
"[thing you blame] isn't the real cause — [other thing] is" contrarian-debunk
formula ("Your Pour-Over Is Sour Because You're Rinsing the Filter Wrong,"
"Surface Tension Is the Real Reason Your Espresso Looks Perfect," "Water
Temperature in Pour-Over Is Overrated. Brew Ratio Is Running the Show.").
Worse, two of those ("The Grinder's Dead Zone: Why Your Burr Alignment
Controls Consistency More Than Burrs Per Minute" and "Burr Alignment Is the
Grinder Spec Nobody Publishes") cover the same underlying topic — burr
alignment as an underrated grinder spec — twice.

Fixes going forward:
- Don't default to the "X isn't the problem, Y is" contrarian-reveal
  structure for every article. Reserve it for when there's a genuinely
  surprising misdiagnosis to correct. Use straight deep-dives, head-to-head
  comparisons, origin/varietal profiles, historical explainers, and honest
  equipment reviews as often as the contrarian format.
- Before finalizing a topic, check it isn't a rehash of an already-published
  angle. Burr alignment vs. burrs-per-minute as a grinder spec has already
  been covered twice — do not write a third.

## Entity checklist for coffee content

Every article should naturally reference relevant specifics from the
following (use what applies — don't force all into every article):

- **Named brewing methods** with variants: V60 (Hario, plastic vs. glass
  vs. ceramic), Kalita Wave (155 vs. 185), Chemex (3-cup vs. 6-cup),
  AeroPress (standard vs. inverted vs. Prismo), French press
- **Named equipment** with model numbers: Baratza Encore ESP, 1Zpresso
  J-Ultra, Fellow Ode Gen 2, Breville Barista Express, Rancilio Silvia,
  Comandante C40 MK4, Timemore Chestnut C3
- **Named origins and varietals**: Ethiopia (Yirgacheffe, Sidamo, Guji),
  Colombia (Huila, Antioquia), Kenya AA/AB, Panama Geisha, Bourbon vs.
  Typica vs. SL28 vs. Geisha vs. Pacamara
- **Processing methods** by name: washed, natural, honey (yellow/red/black),
  wet-hulled (giling basah), anaerobic fermentation
- **Extraction variables** with actual numbers: brew ratio (1:15, 1:16,
  1:17), grind size (with reference: "12 clicks on a Comandante"), water
  temp (90°C, 93°C, 96°C), TDS (1.2–1.4% for pour-over, 8–12% for
  espresso), extraction yield (18–22%)
- **Named institutions**: SCA (Specialty Coffee Association), World
  Coffee Research, CoE (Cup of Excellence), COE growers program, ICO
  (International Coffee Organization)
- **Named people and named books**: Peter Giuliano, Matt Perger, James
  Hoffmann, Scott Rao (*Everything but Espresso*, *The Coffee Roaster's
  Companion*), Talor Browne
- **Common defects by name**: sour (under-extraction), bitter/ashy
  (over-extraction), papery (unrinsed filter), quakers, phenolic, baggy
- **Comparable alternatives**: 1–3 other named methods, machines, or beans
  the reader can use as reference points
- **Time and price markers**: "released in 2023," "$180 at time of
  writing" — real dates, real prices, not "current" or "affordable"
