# Cat Fancast — Site Description for Content Generation

## What this site is

**Cat Fancast** is a cat enthusiast site and editorial blog for real cats. It covers
the world of domestic cats with the voice of an engaged, knowledgeable enthusiast —
not a detached vet-school textbook, not a surface-level pet-care aggregator, not a
breeder catalog. Readers are cat owners, prospective owners, and curious readers who
want to understand the cats in their lives more deeply.

The site is intentionally about **real cats only**. Fictional/copyrighted cat
characters are out of scope — see "What NOT to write" below.

## What to write

Every article must center on **a specific breed, a specific behavior, a specific
biological question, a specific health or care topic, or a specific real cat from
history or culture**. The site's value is in going beneath the surface of one
concrete thing, not in producing generic "cats are great" content.

### Green-light topics

- **Breed deep-dives**: a specific breed's origins, defining traits, the genetics or
  selective-breeding history behind those traits, common health concerns, what a
  prospective owner actually signs up for.
  - Good: "Maine Coon Size: The Genetics and History Behind the Largest Domestic Breed"
  - Good: "Why Bengals Are So High-Energy: The Wild Ancestor Half of the Breed"

- **Behavior & psychology**: a specific named behavior — purring, kneading, the slow
  blink, head-bunting, the bunny-kick, chattering at birds — explained from the
  research and ethology, not folklore. Root every claim in named behaviors and the
  best available science (peer-reviewed studies, named researchers when applicable).
  - Good: "What the Slow Blink Actually Means, According to the Research"
  - Good: "Why Cats Knead: Three Competing Explanations and What the Evidence Says"

- **Biology & science**: how cats actually work — night vision, whisker function,
  the righting reflex, the cat genome, coat-color genetics, the domestication
  timeline. Anchor every claim in specifics (named studies, named anatomical
  structures, real numbers).
  - Good: "How Cat Night Vision Actually Works: The Tapetum Lucidum Explained"
  - Good: "Why Calico Cats Are Almost Always Female: The X-Inactivation Story"

- **Health & care**: a specific named condition, life stage, or care concern —
  chronic kidney disease, FIP, dental disease, indoor enrichment, multi-cat
  introductions, senior care, kitten socialization windows. Evergreen framing
  only: never tied to "currently trending" or "this season."
  - Good: "Feline Chronic Kidney Disease: Early Signs Most Owners Miss"
  - Good: "The FIP Breakthrough: How a Once-Fatal Disease Became Treatable"

- **History & culture**: named real cats from history and culture — Tama the
  station master, Larry the Chief Mouser, Stubbs the mayor cat, Dewey Readmore
  Books, Unsinkable Sam — or named historical episodes (Egyptian sacred cats,
  Roman ships' cats, the Black Death and the cat-population myth, the 1871
  Crystal Palace cat show). Real cats and real history only.
  - Good: "Tama the Station Master: How One Cat Saved a Japanese Railway"
  - Good: "Cats in Ancient Egypt: Sacred Animal or Working Pest Control?"

- **Comparative analysis**: which breeds, behaviors, or care approaches differ
  and why — rooted in named, citable specifics rather than vibes.
  - Good: "Russian Blue vs. Chartreux vs. Korat: The Three Grey Breeds Compared"

## What NOT to write

- **Copyrighted or fictional cats**. Hard ban. No articles centered on Garfield,
  Hello Kitty, Tom (from Tom & Jerry), Pusheen, Doraemon, Jiji, the cat from
  Sailor Moon, the Cheshire Cat, Crookshanks, Mrs. Norris, viral-meme cats with
  living rights holders, or any cat character owned by a studio/publisher/creator.
  Articles must be about real cats only. (This is the core reason the site exists
  on a copyright-clean footing.)

- **Generic vet-craft essays disconnected from cats specifically**. Do not write
  standalone explainers on pharmacology, surgery techniques, anesthesia, or
  veterinary education — those belong on a vet-trade site. If clinical material
  is relevant, use it to illuminate a specific feline condition: "how subcutaneous
  fluids slow CKD progression in older cats" is fine; "how to administer
  subcutaneous fluids" as a how-to is not.

- **Non-cat content**. No dogs, no general pet care, no exotic-pet content, no
  big-cat conservation as a standalone topic (a Maine Coon piece may mention
  the lynx-like comparison; a "save the snow leopard" article does not belong).

- **Seasonal, trending, or news-tied angles**. No "this month's cat news,"
  "currently airing cat content," "just released" framing. Cat Fancast is
  ~90% evergreen by design (see TOPIC.md §2).

- **Generic explainers with no named anchor**. Articles like "Why Cats Are
  Popular Pets" or "The History of Cats" are too broad. Every topic must have
  a concrete angle that ties to at least 2–3 named breeds, named real cats,
  named behaviors, or named medical/biological terms.

## Audience

Readers range from first-time owners researching their first cat to long-time
enthusiasts who can name the differences between CFA and TICA breed standards.
Write for the curious owner or prospective owner — assume the reader knows
what a cat is, what a breed is, what a vet is, and what spaying/neutering
means. Don't assume they already know the specific breed, condition, behavior,
or historical figure-cat you're writing about.

## Tone

Enthusiastic but not breathless. Opinionated but grounded in citable specifics
(CFA/TICA standards, peer-reviewed studies, vet specialty organizations,
named historical sources). The goal is to sound like the most informed cat
person in the room who also genuinely loves the animals — not a sterile
vet-school summary and not a "OMG kitties!!" listicle.

## Entity checklist for cat content

Every article should naturally mention relevant entities from the following
categories (use whatever applies — don't force all categories into every article):

- **Breed name(s)**: the named breed(s) being discussed, with their formal
  recognized registry name when relevant
- **Registries / standards bodies**: CFA (Cat Fanciers' Association), TICA
  (The International Cat Association), GCCF (Governing Council of the Cat
  Fancy), FIFé, when discussing breed standards or recognition history
- **Named real cats**: when relevant — Tama, Larry, Stubbs, Dewey, Unsinkable
  Sam, Grumpy Cat (Tardar Sauce), Lil Bub, etc.
- **Medical / biological terminology**: the real name of the condition,
  anatomical structure, gene, or mechanism (CKD, HCM, FIP, tapetum lucidum,
  vibrissae, Jacobson's organ, X-inactivation, agouti gene)
- **Sources / institutions**: peer-reviewed journals, named researchers, vet
  specialty organizations (AVMA, ISFM, AAFP, ACVIM)
- **Geographic origin**: where a breed actually originated (Maine, Persia/
  Iran, Thailand for Siamese and Korat, Norway, Turkey, etc.)
- **Comparable breeds**: 1–3 other named breeds the reader can use as
  reference points
- **Time markers**: actual dates (year a breed was first recognized, year of
  a study, century for historical entries) rather than "recently"
