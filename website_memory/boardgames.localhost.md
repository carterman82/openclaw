# Board Game Fancast — Site Description for Content Generation

## What this site is

**Board Game Fancast** is a tabletop-gaming editorial site for players who
already own a shelf of games and want to think about them more carefully.
It covers modern hobby board games, classic gateway titles, mechanics,
strategy, and the history of the medium — with the voice of a lifelong
player who reads the rulebook before the first play and takes the game
seriously without taking themselves seriously.

Readers are hobbyists somewhere between "I own Catan and Ticket to Ride
and want to go deeper" and "I have 200 games and an opinion about every
one." They don't need "what is a euro game" explained; they need the
right recommendation for their group, the honest read on the new hotness,
or the strategy insight they missed on the first three plays.

## What to write

Every article must center on **a specific named game, a specific named
designer, a specific mechanic, a specific strategic principle, a specific
type of gaming session (2-player, family, party, campaign), or a specific
piece of the medium's history**. The value is in going deep on one
concrete thing, not in producing generic "board games are back!" content.

### Green-light topics

- **Game deep-dives**: a specific named game — Terraforming Mars, Wingspan,
  Brass: Birmingham, Spirit Island, 7 Wonders Duel, Root, Ark Nova — with
  its designer, publisher, mechanic backbone, player-count sweet spot,
  strategic core, common failure modes on first play, and honest take on
  who it's for.
  - Good: "Brass: Birmingham Strategy: Why Your Opening Loans Decide the Game"
  - Good: "Spirit Island Solo vs. Two-Handed vs. Group: Which Actually Plays Best"

- **Designer profiles**: named designers and their design DNA — Vital
  Lacerda, Uwe Rosenberg, Cole Wehrle, Jamey Stegmaier, Reiner Knizia,
  Elizabeth Hargrave. What they optimize for, what their games share, and
  where a reader who loves one should look next.
  - Good: "Uwe Rosenberg's Signature: Why His Games All Feel Like Home"
  - Good: "Cole Wehrle Games Ranked: The Asymmetric-Faction Auteur"

- **Mechanics**: a specific named mechanic — worker placement, deck
  building, action drafting, rondel, engine building, area control,
  legacy campaigns, tableau building, negotiation. Explain the design
  problem the mechanic solves and cite 3–5 named games that use it well.
  - Good: "Rondel Games Explained: Why the Wheel Is Making a Comeback"
  - Good: "Worker Placement vs. Action Selection: The Distinction That Matters"

- **Strategy**: a specific strategic principle applied to a specific game —
  opening moves in Terraforming Mars, resource curves in Wingspan, timing
  the bomb in Terraforming Mars corp draft, blocking in Concordia, when to
  scoop in Root. Named game, named strategic idea, worked example.
  - Good: "Terraforming Mars: The Prelude Opening That Actually Works"
  - Good: "Wingspan Habitat Balance: The Math Behind a Consistent Egg Engine"

- **Reviews & comparisons**: head-to-head reviews of specific named games
  in a category. Opinionated. Pick winners for specific use cases (best
  2-player, best 4-hour euro, best filler at 30 min, best gateway for a
  new-to-hobby group).
  - Good: "The Best 2-Player Board Games at Under 45 Minutes"
  - Good: "Wingspan vs. Everdell vs. Ark Nova: Three Engine Builders Compared"

- **Rules & how-to-play**: a specific named rules question or common
  misplay — end-game triggers in Race for the Galaxy, agenda phase in
  Twilight Imperium, contested drafts in 7 Wonders, cascade rules in
  Splendor. Answer the actual question, cite the rulebook page, and note
  the common houserule if relevant.
  - Good: "The 7 Wonders Rule Everyone Gets Wrong: Trade at Both Neighbors"

- **History & culture**: named historical episodes — the German-designer
  wave (Klaus Teuber and Settlers, 1995), the Kickstarter explosion
  (2012–), the rise of Kennerspiel des Jahres, the CCG-to-LCG shift, the
  Cones of Dunshire meme's real Pandemic Legacy roots. Real games, real
  history, named dates.
  - Good: "The Spiel des Jahres: 40 Years of the Award That Shapes the Hobby"
  - Good: "The Kickstarter Board Game Era: How Everything Changed After Kingdom Death: Monster"

- **Comparative analysis**: which games, mechanics, or designers differ
  and why — rooted in named, citable specifics.
  - Good: "Root vs. Oath: How Cole Wehrle Learned From His Own Design"

## What NOT to write

- **News-tied or trending posts**. No "Best Games of Essen 2026," no "5
  Kickstarters Live This Week," no "This week in board games." Evergreen
  framing only. If a game is on the site, it's because someone will still
  want to know about it in three years.

- **RPGs, wargames, or video games**. Board Game Fancast is about hobby
  board games and their close cousins (deck builders, roll-and-writes,
  narrative campaign games). D&D, Warhammer 40K, chess opening theory,
  and video-game articles belong on other sites.

- **Chess and Go strategy at expert level**. Introducing chess or Go to a
  hobby-board-gamer audience is fine ("if you like Hive, try Go"). Deep
  opening-theory or endgame-tablebase content belongs on dedicated chess
  and Go sites.

- **Political/tribal reviews**. Don't publish "this game is bad because
  its theme is problematic" without engaging the actual play experience,
  and don't publish "this game is above criticism because it won a big
  award." Every review must engage what happens at the table.

- **Sponsored-content veneer**. No "5 amazing games from Publisher X"
  disguised roundups. If we're comparing publishers, name it and be
  honest about differences.

- **Generic explainers with no named anchor**. "Why Board Games Are Fun"
  or "The Rise of Modern Board Games" without specific titles, designers,
  and dates has no place here.

## Audience

Readers are hobbyists somewhere between 25 and 55 (with a long tail on
either side). They already know what a euro is, what worker placement is,
what BGG is, and what a designer credit means. They don't need
gaming-101 explanations. They need honest recommendations, mechanic
insight, and strategy that helps them enjoy their existing shelf more.

Play-group context varies: solo, couple, family with kids, weekly game
night with the same 4 people, occasional 6+ party. Name the context you're
writing to.

## Tone

Conversational, opinionated, informed. Use the second person ("you"). Sound
like the person at your local game shop who's played 500 games, remembers
which ones actually delivered, and will tell you the truth about the new
release even if the internet loves it.

Humor is welcome when it's earned — a wry aside about analysis paralysis
lands; a laugh-track "board games amirite" quip doesn't. When a beloved
game has a real flaw, name it. When a critically panned game does one
thing well, name that too.

## Entity checklist for content

Every article should naturally reference relevant specifics from the
following (use what applies — don't force all into every article):

- **Named games** with publication years: "Brass: Birmingham (Roxley,
  2018)," "Wingspan (Stonemaier, 2019)," "Twilight Imperium 4th ed. (FFG,
  2017)"
- **Named designers**: Vital Lacerda, Uwe Rosenberg, Cole Wehrle, Reiner
  Knizia, Elizabeth Hargrave, Isaac Childres, Klaus Teuber, Vlaada Chvátil
- **Named publishers**: Stonemaier, Fantasy Flight, Days of Wonder, CGE,
  Roxley, Leder Games, Feuerland, Awaken Realms
- **BGG references** when useful: "BGG rank #7 as of writing," "BGG weight
  4.2/5" — but only when the number actually helps the reader
- **Awards & recognition**: Spiel des Jahres, Kennerspiel des Jahres, Golden
  Geek, Diana Jones — with year and category when cited
- **Mechanics** by name: worker placement, deck building, action drafting,
  rondel, area control, engine building, tableau building, hand management,
  set collection, variable player powers, legacy
- **Player counts** honestly: "shines at 3," "4p is the sweet spot," "solo
  mode is bolted-on"
- **Play length** honestly: "45 min with experienced players, 90 min for
  the first play"
- **Comparable games**: 1–3 other named titles the reader can use as
  reference points
- **Time markers**: actual publication years, actual award years, real
  edition numbers — not "recently released" or "the current version"
