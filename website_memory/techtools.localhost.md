# Tech Tool Guide — Site Description for Content Generation

## What this site is

**Tech Tool Guide** is a practical editorial site for founders, freelancers,
marketers, and small business operators who live in software every day. It covers
SaaS products, AI tools, productivity systems, and business strategy with the
voice of a well-informed practitioner — not a detached analyst report, not a
vendor press release, not a shallow listicle padded to hit a word count.

Readers are people who are actively choosing, using, or switching tools. They
have real jobs to do and need information that helps them decide faster and use
software better.

## What to write

Every article must center on **a specific tool, a specific business problem
software can solve, a specific category of SaaS products, a specific workflow
pattern, or a specific business skill that intersects with software use**. The
site's value is in going beneath the surface of one concrete thing, not
producing generic "technology is changing business" commentary.

### Green-light topics

- **AI Tools**: specific AI products (writing assistants, image generators,
  meeting summarizers, coding tools), use-case breakdowns, honest
  capability/limitation assessments, workflows that combine AI tools
  effectively.
  - Good: "How to Use AI Writing Tools Without Sounding Like a Robot"
  - Good: "The Best AI Meeting Summarizers Compared: Otter vs. Fireflies vs. Grain"

- **Automation & Workflows**: specific automations founders and small teams
  actually run — Zapier flows, Make (Integromat) scenarios, CRM-to-email
  triggers, invoice automation, client onboarding pipelines. Every article
  names the specific tools in the chain.
  - Good: "How to Automate Client Onboarding With Notion, Calendly, and Zapier"
  - Good: "5 Time-Saving Automations Every Freelancer Should Set Up in Zapier"

- **Best Of / Comparisons**: head-to-head reviews and ranked roundups of real
  named tools in a specific category. Must be opinionated: pick winners,
  explain trade-offs, state who each tool is best for. No fencesitting.
  - Good: "The Best Project Management Tools for Small Teams in 2024"
  - Good: "Notion vs. Obsidian vs. Roam: Which Note-Taking Tool Actually Sticks?"

- **Business skills & strategy**: the operational and strategic decisions that
  come with running or growing a business — hiring, pricing, positioning,
  team structure, growth strategy, failure modes. Ground every claim in named
  frameworks, named business thinkers, or specific real-world examples.
  - Good: "Why Startups Fail: The 11 Patterns That Repeat Across Failed Companies"
  - Good: "Product-Led Growth vs. Sales-Led Growth: When Each Model Wins"

- **Design**: practical design decisions for people who aren't professional
  designers — choosing a color palette for a brand, making a landing page
  convert better, picking a font pairing, using Figma or Canva effectively.
  - Good: "How to Choose Brand Colors That Actually Work (Without a Designer)"
  - Good: "The Landing Page Anatomy That Converts: 7 Elements, in Order"

- **Marketing**: channel strategy, copywriting, email marketing, content
  marketing, SEO for small teams. Specific tactics and named tools, not
  vague advice.
  - Good: "Cold Email Formulas That Get Replies: The Framework Behind High-Response Outreach"
  - Good: "How to Set Up a 5-Email Welcome Sequence That Actually Onboards People"

- **Freelance**: client acquisition, scoping, pricing, contracts, retainers,
  client communication, rate setting, getting off Upwork. Honest and specific.
  - Good: "How to Price Your Freelance Services Without Undercharging"
  - Good: "The Freelance Contract Clauses That Protect You From Scope Creep"

- **Productivity Tools**: task managers, note-taking apps, calendar/scheduling
  tools, focus apps, time-tracking. Review and compare specific named apps,
  not the abstract concept of productivity.
  - Good: "Todoist vs. TickTick vs. Things 3: A Real-World Productivity Tool Comparison"
  - Good: "How to Use Time Blocking With Google Calendar Without It Falling Apart"

- **SaaS / Guides & Tutorials**: step-by-step how-to content on using specific
  named SaaS products effectively; onboarding patterns; feature deep-dives;
  workflows insiders use that most users miss.
  - Good: "The Notion Setup That Actually Gets Used: A Minimalist Workspace Guide"
  - Good: "How to Set Up HubSpot CRM for a 5-Person Sales Team From Scratch"

## What NOT to write

- **Generic technology hype**. Articles like "How AI Is Changing Everything" or
  "The Future of Work Is Here" without specific tools, workflows, or actionable
  takeaways are out. Every abstract claim needs a concrete anchor.

- **Product announcements or news**. No "Company X Just Released Feature Y"
  coverage. Keep it evergreen — the article should be as useful in two years
  as the day it publishes.

- **Consumer-facing tech** not relevant to business operators. No smartphone
  reviews, gaming gear, consumer apps (Instagram tips, TikTok dancing), or
  entertainment software. The reader is running something, not browsing.

- **Enterprise-only software** with no small-team or freelance relevance.
  Salesforce implementation guides for Fortune 500 sales orgs, SAP, Oracle,
  large-scale IT infrastructure — out of scope. The audience is founders,
  small teams, freelancers, and solo operators.

- **Opinion without substance**. No "10 Mindset Shifts to Unlock Your
  Potential" motivational content. Every article must give the reader
  something to *do* or *decide* with specific named tools or methods.

- **Duplicate angles already on the site**. Check recent post titles before
  generating. Don't publish a second "best project management tools" article
  if one already exists — find a narrower or different angle instead.

## Audience

Readers are practical people in the 25–45 range: freelancers, solo founders,
small agency operators, marketing managers at startups, operations leads at
growing teams. They already know what SaaS is, what a CRM is, what automation
means. They don't need software concepts explained from first principles — they
need specific, honest, actionable guidance on which tools to choose, how to
set them up, and how to get real results without a six-figure IT budget.

Write for someone who is smart, time-short, and has been burned by vague advice
before. Cut to the insight faster than they'd expect.

## Tone

Direct and practical, not corporate. Opinionated — state what's actually better
and why. Use the second person ("you") throughout. Write like a sharp colleague
who has already done the research and is giving a straight recommendation, not
a consultant padding a report.

Avoid buzzwords: "game-changer," "holistic," "synergy," "leverage" (as a verb),
"unlock your potential," "next level." Say the specific thing instead.

Humor is welcome when it's dry and earned, not when it's performative. If in
doubt, be clearer instead of funnier.

## Entity checklist for content

Every article should naturally reference relevant specifics from the following
(use what applies — don't force all into every article):

- **Named tools**: the specific software products being discussed, with their
  current official names (Zapier not "automation platform," Notion not
  "wiki tool," Claude not "an AI")
- **Pricing tiers**: when comparing tools, naming the actual plan (e.g.
  "Zapier's free plan," "Notion's Plus tier," "HubSpot's Starter at $45/mo")
  grounds the reader in real decisions
- **Named frameworks or methodologies**: Jobs-to-be-Done, OKRs, Getting Things
  Done (GTD), PARA method, Agile/Scrum when relevant — never invented jargon
- **Named companies or founders**: real examples, not composites ("Basecamp's
  approach to async work" beats "some companies have found that…")
- **Specific numbers**: conversion rates, pricing, time estimates, user counts
  when verifiable — specificity signals authority
- **Comparison alternatives**: 2–4 other named tools the reader can use as
  reference points when the article recommends one over others
- **Use-case callouts**: who each tool or approach is best for — "best for
  solo freelancers," "better if your team is already in Google Workspace," etc.
